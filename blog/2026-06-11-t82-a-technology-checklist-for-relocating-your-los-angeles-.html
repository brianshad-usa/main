Tech Checklist for Business Relocation

By Brian Shad  ·  Pro Link Systems  ·  June 11, 2026

Relocating your business is an exciting milestone, but it comes with significant challenges—especially when it comes to your technology infrastructure. A poorly planned IT move can result in costly downtime, lost data, and frustrated employees and customers. Whether you're moving across Woodland Hills or to a different part of Los Angeles, having a comprehensive technology checklist is essential for a smooth transition.

At Pro Link Systems, we've helped countless Los Angeles businesses successfully relocate since 1999. Here's our expert technology checklist to ensure your move goes off without a hitch.

Start with a Comprehensive IT Audit and Planning Phase

Before packing a single cable, you need to understand exactly what technology assets you have and how they'll fit into your new space. Begin your relocation planning at least three months in advance to avoid last-minute complications.

Your initial audit should include:

  • Complete inventory: Document all hardware, including servers, workstations, printers, phones, networking equipment, and peripherals
  • Software licenses and subscriptions: Create a master list of all software, cloud services, and their renewal dates
  • Network documentation: Map your current network infrastructure, including cabling, switches, routers, and Wi-Fi access points
  • Vendor agreements: Review contracts with internet service providers, phone systems, and other technology vendors
  • Security systems: Note all access control systems, security cameras, and alarm systems that need relocation

This audit phase is also the perfect time to evaluate what's working and what isn't. Consider upgrading outdated equipment or transitioning to cloud-based solutions that make future moves easier. Partnering with a provider of managed IT services Los Angeles businesses trust can help you identify opportunities for modernization during this transition.

Coordinate Infrastructure Setup at Your New Location

Your new office needs to be technology-ready before moving day. This requires careful coordination with multiple vendors and service providers, often several weeks in advance.

Key infrastructure considerations include:

  • Internet and telecommunications: Contact your ISP at least 4-6 weeks before your move to schedule installation at the new location. If you're changing providers, allow even more time for setup and testing
  • Electrical requirements: Ensure your new space has adequate power outlets and circuits for your equipment, including dedicated circuits for servers and networking hardware
  • Network cabling: Plan your network infrastructure based on your new floor plan, including Ethernet runs, patch panels, and Wi-Fi coverage
  • Server room or data closet: Prepare a secure, climate-controlled space for critical infrastructure with proper ventilation and access controls
  • Phone systems: Configure your phone system at the new location and update your business contact information across all platforms

Testing your infrastructure before the official move date is crucial. If possible, set up core systems at the new location and run them parallel to your existing setup to identify and resolve issues before they impact your business operations.

Protect Your Data and Plan for Business Continuity

Data loss during a business move is more common than you might think. Equipment gets damaged, hard drives fail under the stress of transportation, and human error can lead to accidentally deleted files. Your data protection strategy should be airtight during this vulnerable period.

Essential data protection steps include:

  • Complete backups: Perform full backups of all servers, workstations, and critical data at least one week before the move, and again the night before
  • Cloud synchronization: Ensure all cloud-based data is fully synced and accessible from any location
  • Backup verification: Test your backups to confirm they can be successfully restored if needed
  • Secure transportation: Transport servers and backup drives separately to minimize risk, and use professional IT equipment movers when possible
  • Offsite storage: Maintain copies of critical data in an offsite or cloud location throughout the moving process

You should also develop a business continuity plan that addresses how your team will work during the transition. Will employees work remotely on moving day? How will customers reach you? Having clear answers to these questions minimizes disruption to your operations.

Execute a Systematic Moving Day Plan

Moving day requires careful choreography to ensure everything arrives safely and gets set up in the correct order. Your IT infrastructure typically needs to come online in a specific sequence to function properly.

Follow this systematic approach:

  • Label everything: Clearly mark all equipment, cables, and components with their destination locations and connection details
  • Photograph connections: Take photos of cable connections before unplugging anything to simplify reassembly
  • Pack strategically: Use original packaging when available, and clearly mark fragile equipment
  • Designate an IT point person: Have a knowledgeable team member or your IT support Los Angeles partner oversee the technology aspects of the move
  • Prioritize setup sequence: Establish internet connectivity first, followed by servers, then workstations and peripherals
  • Test systematically: Check each system as it comes online before moving to the next component

Consider scheduling your physical move over a weekend or during off-hours to minimize impact on your business operations. This gives your team time to set up and test systems before employees need to be productive in the new space.
