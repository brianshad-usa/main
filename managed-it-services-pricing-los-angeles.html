Section 1

Pricing models explained

The model you choose shapes every invoice you receive. Here are the four structures you will encounter in the LA market.

Per-User Flat Rate

The most common and transparent model. You pay a fixed monthly fee per employee, and everything is included — help desk, monitoring, security, backup. Predictable budgeting, no per-ticket charges, and no penalty for calling support frequently. This is the model we recommend for most businesses.

Per-Device Pricing

A fee is charged for each managed device — workstations, servers, network equipment. Can work well for device-heavy environments like manufacturing or warehouses, but costs grow quickly as employees use multiple devices and the model is harder to forecast as your business changes.

Tiered Service Levels

Providers offer Basic, Standard, and Premium tiers at different price points, with services stacked accordingly. Useful when different teams have different needs. The key is ensuring the base tier is genuinely useful — not so stripped-down that you end up on Premium anyway.

Break-Fix / Hourly

You call when something breaks and pay an hourly rate — typically $125-250/hour in Los Angeles. No ongoing relationship, no proactive monitoring, no vested interest in preventing problems. Fine for one-off projects, but a poor fit for businesses that rely on IT day to day. Costs are unpredictable and can spike badly during outages.

Section 2

What affects the price

Two businesses with the same headcount can have very different managed IT costs. The following factors have the biggest impact on your monthly investment:

  • Number of users and devices. More users means more licenses, more help desk volume, and more endpoints to secure. Server infrastructure adds cost as well.
  • Compliance requirements. HIPAA, PCI-DSS, and similar frameworks require specific controls, documentation, and audit support — all of which add meaningful time and complexity to a managed IT engagement.
  • Cloud vs. on-premise infrastructure. Cloud-first environments are generally easier and less expensive to manage. On-premise servers and hybrid environments require more hands-on work and specialized skills.
  • Number of locations. Each office or site needs its own network monitoring, security, and potentially on-site support coverage, which increases scope.
  • 24/7 vs. business-hours support. Around-the-clock help desk coverage costs more than business-hours-only support. For industries like healthcare and logistics, 24/7 is non-negotiable.
  • Included cybersecurity vs. add-on. Some providers advertise a low base price and then charge separately for EDR, email security, and SOC services. A true all-inclusive plan already bundles these in.

Section 4

Red flags in IT pricing

Not all managed IT proposals are created equal. These are the warning signs that a low headline price will cost you more in the long run:

  • Per-hour billing for ongoing support. Hourly billing creates a structural incentive to take longer to resolve issues. A managed IT provider should be motivated to fix things fast — flat-rate pricing aligns that incentive.
  • Low base price with many add-ons. A $60/user quote that doesn't include backup, security, or after-hours support is not a $60/user plan. Always ask for a fully loaded price that includes everything you actually need.
  • No transparency on what's included. If a provider won't clearly list what's in scope and what isn't, that ambiguity will cost you money at renewal time or when something goes wrong.
  • Charging for the assessment or discovery call. A reputable MSP should be willing to understand your environment and give you a real quote without charging for the conversation. If they're billing you before you're even a client, that sets the tone for the relationship.
